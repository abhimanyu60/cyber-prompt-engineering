Speaker Notes — Prompt Engineering Lab
Hands-On Training Session · Cybersecurity Team
Confidential · Internal
Session Type
Hands-On Lab (Interactive)
Audience
Security Analysts, Engineers, SOC Team
Total Duration
90 minutes (5 stations)
Room Setup
Participants on laptops, lab open in browser
Prerequisite
Executive briefing session completed
00
Pre-Session Setup & Opening
⏱ 10 min
Before They Arrive — Trainer Checklist
  • Open the lab URL on your own screen and verify all 5 tabs load correctly
  • Ask participants to open the lab on their own laptops — share the GitHub Pages link via Teams chat
  • Confirm M365 Copilot is accessible on their machines (they'll need it for real practice)
  • Have a blank Teams channel or shared doc ready for participants to paste their prompts for group review
  • Print or have your speaker notes visible on a second screen — don't leave these open on the projector
Opening — What to Say
"The session we just came from was about understanding how prompt engineering works. This session is about doing it. You'll write real prompts, get them diagnosed, make mistakes, and fix them — that's the point. There's no marking, there's no wrong answer that I care about. What matters is that by the end, you have a set of prompts you can actually use tomorrow morning."

"The lab has five stations. We'll work through them together but at your own pace. When you're ahead, explore. When you're stuck, shout — that's what I'm here for."
Trainer Tip — Set the Right Expectation
This audience has just seen a CISO-level briefing. Some will arrive confident, some intimidated. Normalise that their first prompts will be weak — that's the point of Station 3 (Prompt Doctor). Tell them explicitly: "The worst prompts make the best learning moments."
01
RCTF Builder
⏱ 15 min
Station Intent
This is the warm-up. The goal is muscle memory — getting participants to instinctively think in four components before they write a single word. The live assembly and score bar give them immediate dopamine feedback. Let it run.
What to Say — Introducing the Station
"Open Station 1 — RCTF Builder. You'll see four boxes. Don't think too hard — pick any security task you do regularly and try to write a prompt for it using these four boxes. Start typing and watch what happens at the bottom."

Give them 5 minutes to free-type. Then:

"Now click one of the Quick Starters at the bottom — pick whichever is closest to your work. See how it fills in. This is a template, not a final prompt — your job is to replace the brackets with your actual environment details."
Facilitation — What to Do
Walk the room while they type. Look for two things:

1. The Format field left blank — this is the most common gap. When you see it, don't tell them — ask: "If Copilot gave you this analysis as a 500-word essay, would that be useful?" They'll add a format themselves.

2. Vague Tasks — someone will write "analyse the alerts" in the Task box. Ask: "What decision should the AI help you make? What would you do differently based on the output?"
Watch For — Common Mistakes
Putting everything in the Task box — participant combines role + context + task into one paragraph. They're thinking like a Google search, not a structured brief. Gently point out the four fields and ask them to split it out.

Score hits 75% and they stop — remind them the Format and Constraints fields push it to 100% and that's when prompts stop needing rework after Copilot responds.
Common Questions
Does every prompt need all four components?
For a quick one-off question — no. But for anything that feeds into a report, a decision, or gets shared with leadership — yes, always. Think of RCTF as the standard for security-grade prompts, not casual use.
How long should a prompt be?
As long as it needs to be — but not longer. A well-written RCTF prompt is usually 80–150 words. If it's longer than a paragraph per section, you're probably over-explaining. Let the format do the work.
Can I save these prompts somewhere?
Yes — that's what we'll cover in the Cheat Sheet station. For now, use the Copy button and paste your best ones into a OneNote or Teams message to yourself.
Move to Station 2 after ~15 minutes. You don't need everyone perfect — move when most are at 75%+ on the score bar.
02
Scenario Lab
⏱ 30 min
Station Intent
This is the core of the session. Participants write prompts for real security situations under light time pressure. The feedback button gives them structured critique without you having to review every individual prompt. The model answer is the debrief tool — use it to facilitate group discussion, not to show off a "right answer."
What to Say — Introducing Scenarios
"Open Station 2. You have five scenarios — pick the one closest to your daily work and start there. Read the situation, write your prompt in the box, then hit Analyse My Prompt. Don't look at the model answer yet — that's your reward after you've had a go."

"You've got about 6 minutes per scenario. I'd rather you do two well than five quickly. Go."
Facilitation by Scenario
Scenario 1 · Phishing Wave
Most participants will nail the task but forget to add constraints around legal language ("flag patterns only — do not assume breach"). When you debrief this one, highlight how missing that constraint could produce AI output that a Legal team would flag as premature conclusion. Real consequence = real learning.
Scenario 2 · Insider Threat DLP Review
This is the most legally sensitive scenario. Watch for participants who write prompts that say "determine if the user is stealing data." Stop and address with the group: "What happens if this AI output is shown to HR or Legal and it contains the word 'stealing'?" The correct language is patterns, not conclusions. This is the one scenario where constraints are not optional.
Scenario 3 · Ransomware IOC Chain
Most participants will write one big prompt. That's fine — let the analysis tool flag it, then ask: "What happens if Step 1 produces bad data and you feed it into Steps 2 and 3 without checking?" This surfaces the verifiability value of chaining — each step is a checkpoint.
Scenario 4 · Threat Intel Triage
Key teaching moment: ask participants to read back their environment profile section. Most will have written it generically. Ask: "If you paste this prompt without your actual stack, what does Copilot use as the environment? Its best guess. Is that good enough for a financial services threat assessment?"
Scenario 5 · Board Brief
Save this for your strongest participants or as a group exercise. Show both their prompt and the model answer on the projector and ask: "What's different? Why does the model answer explicitly instruct 'do not speculate on attacker identity'?" This ties back to the hallucination slide from the executive session.
Trainer Tip — Group Debrief Technique
After each scenario, ask one participant to share their prompt on the projector. Do not critique the prompt yourself first. Instead ask the group: "What's strong here? What's missing?" Let peers give feedback — it lands better than trainer feedback and builds shared vocabulary around RCTF.
Watch For — The Shortcut Trap
Some participants will click "Show Model Answer" immediately without writing their own prompt. That's fine as a reference — but call it out lightly: "The model answer is the ceiling, not the floor. If you read it first, you're just learning to copy. Write yours first, even if it's rough — that's where the learning happens."
Common Questions
Do I have to do all 5 scenarios?
No — depth over breadth. Two scenarios done properly, with a model answer comparison and group discussion, will give more value than five done superficially.
Can I use these prompts in Copilot right now to test them?
Yes — that's the ideal. Copy your prompt, paste it into Copilot, and paste a sample (C2-classified) alert or email as the data. That's the full workflow. If you get a bad output, come back and use the Prompt Doctor to diagnose why.
What if Copilot gives a completely different answer to what the model answer suggests?
That's expected — the model answers here are reference prompts, not guaranteed outputs. What matters is whether your prompt produced something useful and accurate. If Copilot's answer is better, note what your prompt did differently.
Move to Station 3 after 30 minutes. If the group is energised on scenarios, let them continue while you introduce Prompt Doctor as the "self-service critique tool" they can use going forward.
03
Prompt Doctor
⏱ 10 min
Station Intent
This station teaches self-sufficiency. After today, participants won't have a trainer to review their prompts. The Prompt Doctor is a habit-forming tool — show them how to use it on their own prompts before they use it on the sample. That creates personal relevance.
What to Say
"Go to Station 3 — Prompt Doctor. Don't click the sample prompt button yet. Take one of the prompts you wrote in Station 2 — the one you're least confident about — and paste it here. Hit Run Diagnosis."

Let them see their own score. Then:

"Now click Load Sample — this is a deliberately bad prompt. Compare your score to the sample. Whatever the Doctor flags, that's the exact thing to fix before you use a prompt in production."
Trainer Tip — The Hallucination Guard Check
Most participants will score low on Hallucination Guard — this is intentional. Use it as a bridge to Station 4. Say: "Notice the Hallucination Guard check. Almost everyone misses this one. Station 4 is going to show you exactly why it matters — with some scenarios that will make you uncomfortable about how much you've trusted AI output before today."
Quick Facilitation
This station is self-service — you don't need to run it. Circulate and ask individuals: "What did it flag on yours?" Then ask them to fix the prompt and re-run. Two iterations is enough — the pattern clicks fast once they see it twice.
Station 3 is intentionally quick. Move to Station 4 as a whole group — the quiz works better if everyone does it together so you can debrief answers live.
04
Hallucination Quiz
⏱ 15 min
Station Intent
This is the most memorable station. The quiz uses competitive instinct (score visible, reset available) to surface complacency about AI output. The goal is one specific behaviour change: participants stop treating AI output as verified fact without cross-checking. Each question is designed to create a "wait — I would have trusted that" moment.
What to Say — Introducing the Quiz
"Station 4. Six questions. Do them individually — don't discuss yet. You'll see the explanation after each answer. We'll debrief as a group at the end. Ready? Go."

Give them 5–6 minutes to complete independently. Then bring the group together for debrief.
Debrief — Question by Question
Q1 — Fake CVE ID
Ask: "Hands up who would have used that CVE reference in a patch request without checking?" Usually 30–50% raise hands. That's the moment. Say: "That's not a mistake — that's what AI is designed to do. It sounds credible because it is credible-sounding. The only defence is verification as a habit, not an afterthought."
Q2 — Confidence Hallucination on Attribution
This trips almost everyone. Reinforce: "The original said 'suspected.' AI said 'high confidence.' That upgrade happened silently. If that goes into a regulatory notification, you've made a statement you can't support." The constraint to add: "Preserve the confidence level from the source — do not upgrade uncertainty to certainty."
Q3 — GDPR Clause (Correct but Still Verify)
This one is intentionally a near-miss — the AI was right. Say: "This time it was correct. The problem is you had no way of knowing that without checking. The verification habit has to be consistent — you can't only check AI when you're suspicious. You check it because it's AI."
Q4 — Wrong EventID (Most Impactful)
This is the strongest moment in the quiz. Ask: "If that SIEM rule went into production — what happens? The rule fires on wrong events, or never fires at all, and you think you have coverage you don't." Pause. Let it land. Then: "That's a false sense of security. That's worse than no rule."
Q5 — Summarisation Risk
A more nuanced risk — AI is generally good at summarisation but can silently drop caveats. Give them the practical rule: "Two minutes of spot-checking saves two hours of backtracking if a key nuance was dropped."
Q6 — The Gold Standard Constraint
The best answer is C — grounding the AI in provided data. Use this to bridge into the Cheat Sheet: "This constraint is already in your Cheat Sheet under the Constraint Toolkit. It should go into every analysis prompt you write from today."
Watch For — Defensive Reactions
Some participants may feel called out, especially if they've already been using Copilot heavily. If the room gets quiet after Q4, normalise it: "Nobody in this room — including me — has been checking every EventID. This isn't a blame exercise. It's about building the habit before it costs us something we can't undo."
After quiz debrief, move directly to Station 5. Frame it as: "Now that you know what not to trust, here's your reference for building prompts you can trust."
05
Cheat Sheet
⏱ 10 min
Station Intent
This is the "take home" station. Participants don't need to do anything here — they need to understand what exists and how to use it. The goal is for every participant to leave with at least one template copied and adapted for their actual work before the session ends.
What to Say
"Station 5 is your reference card. You don't memorise this — you keep the tab open. The data classification table is your first check before every Copilot session. The constraint toolkit is your prompt review list. The templates are starting points — not finished prompts. Take 5 minutes and copy the template most relevant to your role, adapt the brackets to your actual stack, and save it somewhere you'll actually find it."
Action — Make Them Commit to One Template
Before closing the station, ask each participant: "Which template did you save? What did you change in it to make it yours?" Go around the room. This takes 3 minutes but creates accountability. A template they've adapted once is one they'll use. An untouched template gets forgotten.
Trainer Tip — The Version Control Moment
Point to the Prompt Library — Treat It as an Organisational Asset card in the Cheat Sheet. Say: "The prompts you write today — the good ones — should not live only on your laptop. Put them in a shared Git repo or SharePoint page. Next quarter, when the threat landscape changes, you update the prompt, not write from scratch. That's a prompt library. That's what turns individual knowledge into team capability."
06
Session Close & Commitments
⏱ 10 min
Closing Words
"You've built prompts today that are better than what most security teams are using right now. That's not a compliment — it's a low bar. The industry is still figuring this out. What you do with this in the next 30 days is what matters."

"Three things I want you to leave with. One — RCTF. Every prompt, every time. Two — the hallucination guard constraint in every analysis. Three — one template adapted and saved before you leave this room. If you do those three things, the rest will follow."
Commitment Round — Quick Go-Around
Ask each person to state one thing they will do differently by end of next week. Keep it to one sentence each. Write them down — follow up in the team channel. Suggested prompts if they're stuck:

"I'll use RCTF for my [specific recurring task]."
"I'll add a hallucination guard to my threat intel prompts."
"I'll start a prompt library in our team SharePoint / Git repo."
"I'll run the Prompt Doctor before sharing any AI output with leadership."
Follow-Up — Suggested Actions
  • Share the lab link in the team's security channel — it's live on GitHub Pages permanently
  • Create a shared prompt library folder (SharePoint or Git) within the week while momentum is fresh
  • Schedule a 30-minute prompt review in 4 weeks — participants share their best prompt from real work
  • Use Scenario 5 (Board Brief) as a live exercise during the next actual incident — don't wait for training to apply it
Final Questions — Common End-of-Session Questions
Will this lab stay accessible after today?
Yes — it's hosted on GitHub Pages. Share the link in the team channel and bookmark it. The Cheat Sheet is the most useful day-to-day reference.
Can we get more scenarios added for our specific tools (e.g. Splunk, CrowdStrike)?
Yes — the lab is an HTML file in a Git repo. If you want tool-specific scenarios, raise it as a requirement and we can add them in the next update.
How do we know if our prompts are getting better over time?
Two signals: (1) the Prompt Doctor score — aim for 6/7 or 7/7 on any production prompt. (2) Copilot output needs fewer edits before it's shareable. Track it informally — you'll notice within 2 weeks.
Is there a way to share prompts with the wider security community?
Internally — yes, that's the prompt library. Externally — exercise caution. Prompts that reference your stack, crown jewels, or internal systems should stay internal. Sanitise before sharing outside the organisation.
