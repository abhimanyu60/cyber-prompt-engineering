Basics What Is a Prompt? And Why Does It Matter in Cyber? 02 / 14
Core Definition
A prompt is the instruction you give the AI. It is the only lever you have. The quality of your output is directly proportional to the quality of your input. Think of it like a search query — but instead of keywords, you're writing a brief.
Why It Matters for Cyber Teams
Copilot has access to your emails, Teams chats, SharePoint, security alerts. A vague prompt returns vague answers. A precise prompt can surface a phishing pattern across 500 mailboxes in under 10 seconds.
Rule #1: The AI is not reading your mind. Every assumption you don't write, it guesses — and it guesses toward the average, not your edge case.
Bad vs Good Prompt
❌ WEAK PROMPT
Check emails for phishing.
✅ STRONG PROMPT
You are a SOC analyst. Review the last 7 days of emails in the shared Security mailbox. Identify any that contain urgency language, external sender spoofing known internal domains, or suspicious attachment types. List them as a table: Sender | Subject | Risk Indicator | Priority.
Key Difference
The strong prompt defines a role, a data scope, specific signals to look for, and an output format. That's the 4-part anatomy — next slide.
Basics The RCTF Framework — Anatomy of a High-Quality Prompt 03 / 14
R · Role
Tell the AI who it is. This shapes its vocabulary, assumptions, and tone. "You are a senior threat intelligence analyst…"
C · Context
Provide the situation or data. What system? What timeframe? What organisation? The more specific, the sharper the output.
T · Task
State the exact action clearly. "Summarise" is weak. "Classify each alert as Critical / High / Medium / Low with one-line reasoning" is strong.
F · Format
Specify the output structure. Table, bullet points, executive paragraph, JSON — this determines if it's immediately usable or needs reformatting.
Memory trick: R-C-T-F = Role, Context, Task, Format. Every prompt you write should pass a 4-second RCTF check.
Live Example — Alert Triage
RCTF IN ACTION · M365 COPILOT
[R] You are a Tier-1 SOC analyst. [C] I have 12 Microsoft Defender alerts triggered in the last 4 hours across our Azure tenant. They involve unusual sign-in geographies and token reuse. [T] For each alert, determine: Is this a likely account takeover attempt or a false positive? Give your reasoning in one sentence. [F] Return a markdown table: Alert ID | Verdict | Confidence | One-Line Reason
Why This Works
Each bracket maps to a component. The AI knows its authority level (Tier-1), the data scope (12 alerts, 4hr, Azure), the decision it must make, and the exact table format expected. Zero ambiguity.

Intermediate Constraint Layering — Teaching the AI What NOT To Do 05 / 14
Why Constraints Matter
AI has a tendency to be helpful to a fault — it will fill gaps with assumptions. Constraints fence the output: no hallucinated CVEs, no external threat intel that doesn't match your environment, no recommendations that exceed analyst authority.
C1Scope constraint: Only use data from [system/timeframe]
C2Tone constraint: Write for a non-technical executive audience
C3Accuracy constraint: If unsure, say "insufficient data" — do not guess
C4Action constraint: Only recommend actions within Tier-1 analyst authority
C5Length constraint: Maximum 200 words / 5 bullet points
Real Example — Insider Threat Review
CONSTRAINED PROMPT · INSIDER THREAT
You are a DLP (Data Loss Prevention) analyst. Context: The following is a 30-day export of user file-access logs for a user who tendered resignation 2 weeks ago. Task: Identify any anomalous access patterns: bulk downloads, access to data outside normal job scope, after-hours activity, or access to HR/Finance systems. Constraints: - Do NOT make accusations — flag patterns only - Do NOT reference data outside this log - If a behaviour has a benign explanation, note it - Output: Risk summary paragraph + evidence table
Key insight: The constraint "do not make accusations" protects you legally and keeps the AI in its lane — pattern detection, not judgement.
Intermediate Prompt Chaining — Breaking Complex Tasks into Steps 06 / 14
The Concept
One long prompt overloads the AI and gets sloppy results. Instead, chain prompts sequentially — feed the output of prompt 1 into prompt 2. Each step does one thing precisely.
Real Scenario — Ransomware Incident Chain
Step 1 · Extract
"You are a SOC analyst. Extract all IOCs (IPs, hashes, domains) from this incident report. Output as a clean JSON list."
Step 2 · Enrich
"Take this IOC list and map each to known threat actor groups based on your knowledge. Add a confidence score."
Step 3 · Impact
"For each confirmed IOC, describe the potential business impact if this threat actor achieves its objectives in a financial services environment."
Step 4 · Brief
"Convert this enriched intelligence into a 1-page executive brief. Tone: calm, factual, action-first. Avoid jargon."
4×
More precise output per step vs one mega-prompt
~2hr
Average analyst time replaced for post-incident reporting
✓
Each step is auditable — you can verify before proceeding
Intermediate Applied Example — Threat Intelligence Digestion 07 / 14
The Problem
Your team receives 15–20 threat intel feeds daily — vendor bulletins, CISA alerts, ISAC reports. Reading everything is impossible. Copilot can digest all of it and give you the 3 things that actually matter for your environment.
The Technique — Environmental Filtering
Add your technology stack as context. The AI will filter out threats to systems you don't run and surface only what's relevant. This is called environment-anchored prompting.
Pro tip: Create a standard "environment profile" snippet — your stack, your industry, your crown jewels. Paste it at the start of every threat intel prompt for consistent, targeted output.
Walkthrough — Threat Intel Digest
THREAT INTEL PROMPT · INTERMEDIATE
Role: Senior Threat Intelligence Analyst Environment profile: - Industry: Financial Services - Stack: Azure AD, M365, SAP ERP, Linux (RHEL 8), Windows Server 2019 - Key assets: Trading platform, Core Banking, Customer PII DB Task: Review the 3 attached threat bulletins from this week. For each, answer: 1. Does this threat target our stack? 2. What is the likely attack vector? 3. What is the blast radius if successful? 4. What one control would reduce risk most? Format: One card per bulletin. Max 5 lines each. Flag urgency: 🔴🟡🟢

Advanced Chain-of-Thought — Make the AI Reason, Not Just Recall 08 / 14
What Is Chain-of-Thought?
You instruct the AI to "think step by step" before answering. This forces it to reason through a problem rather than pattern-match to a surface-level answer — critical for complex incident analysis where a wrong deduction has real consequences.
When to Use It in Cyber
Attack path analysis · Root cause investigation · Threat actor attribution · Risk quantification · Response prioritisation. Any situation where the reasoning matters as much as the conclusion.
The magic phrase: Add "Think step by step before giving your final answer." or "Walk me through your reasoning." It consistently produces 30–40% more accurate conclusions on complex queries.
Example — Attack Path Reconstruction
CHAIN-OF-THOUGHT · ATTACK ANALYSIS
You are an incident response expert. The following SIEM events occurred over 6 hours: - 03:14 | Brute force on VPN (200 attempts) - 03:47 | Successful login from TOR exit node - 04:02 | Lateral movement to File Server - 04:18 | Large ZIP created in Temp folder - 04:31 | DNS query to unknown external domain - 04:44 | SIEM alert suppressed by admin account Think step by step: 1. What attack stage does each event represent? 2. What is the attacker's likely objective? 3. Which single event was the critical decision point? 4. What was the missed detection opportunity? Show your reasoning for each step before concluding.
Advanced Few-Shot Prompting — Train the Model on Your Standards 09 / 14
The Concept
You give the AI 2–3 examples of what good looks like before asking it to do the real task. It calibrates to your standard — your classification scheme, your language style, your risk thresholds — not its default.
Why It's Powerful for Security Teams
Every organisation defines risk differently. A "High" alert in retail is different from a "High" in banking. Few-shot lets you encode your organisation's risk language into every AI response.
Tip: Build a library of 5–10 example prompts from real past incidents. Paste the relevant example before each new analysis request. This is a 10-minute investment that pays dividends every single day.

Advanced Adversarial Simulation — Think Like the Attacker 10 / 14
The Technique
Assign the AI an adversarial persona. Ask it to attack your environment — from the outside in. Then use the output as a structured red team brief. This is not a real attack — it's a structured thought experiment that surfaces gaps in your defences before a real attacker does.
Scenario — Pre-Pentest Attack Surface Analysis
ADVERSARIAL SIMULATION PROMPT
You are a senior red team operator preparing for an authorised engagement against a financial institution. Target profile: - Azure AD SSO, MFA enabled for 80% of users - Exposed OWA (Outlook Web Access) portal - 3rd party payroll vendor with VPN access - Active social media presence — LinkedIn, Twitter - Known to run SAP on-prem with DMZ exposure Your task: 1. Identify the top 5 attack vectors you would prioritise (ranked by likelihood × impact) 2. For each: describe the initial access technique, the likely lateral movement path, and the target crown jewel 3. Identify which vector is most likely to be undetected by a standard EDR Frame this as intelligence our blue team can use to harden defences. Do not include exploitation code — describe technique categories only (MITRE ATT&CK preferred).

Advanced Executive Brief Automation — From Raw Incident to Board Slide 11 / 14
The Use Case
After a major incident, the CISO needs a board-ready brief in under 2 hours. The problem: analysts write in technical language. Boards speak in risk and business impact. This prompt bridges that gap automatically.
Input 1 · Raw Incident Data
SIEM alerts, ticket notes, raw forensics timeline, affected systems list
Input 2 · Business Context
Regulatory environment (GDPR, RBI, SEBI), customer data involved, revenue systems affected
Output · Executive Brief
What happened (1 sentence) · Business impact · What we did · What's at risk still · What we need from leadership
The Prompt
EXECUTIVE BRIEF GENERATOR
You are a CISO communicating to the Board of Directors. Below is a technical incident summary from our SOC. Our environment is a regulated financial services organisation operating under GDPR and RBI guidelines. Convert this into a Board-level security briefing. Structure it as: 1. HEADLINE (1 sentence — what happened, severity) 2. BUSINESS IMPACT (customer data? revenue? regulatory?) 3. OUR RESPONSE (what we did, timeline) 4. CURRENT STATUS (resolved / monitoring / active) 5. WHAT WE NEED (decisions or resources from leadership) 6. LESSONS LEARNED (1 control we're improving) Tone: Calm. Confident. No jargon. No blame. Action-first. Max 250 words total. Advanced Guardrails & Responsible Use — What Every Analyst Must Know 13 / 14
⚠ Never Put In

• Raw credentials or API keys
• Full customer PII datasets
• Classified incident details not approved for AI processing
• Internal vulnerability scan results in unapproved tools

AI is a processor — treat it like any 3rd party service under your data classification policy.
⚡ Always Verify

• CVE numbers and CVSS scores (hallucinations are real)
• Threat actor attributions (AI may be outdated)
• Regulatory references — always confirm with legal
• Any specific technical recommendation before acting

Copilot is a first-draft engine. A human stays in the loop on every decision.
✓ Best Practices

• Log significant prompts used for audit trail
• Version-control high-value prompt libraries in SharePoint
• Review AI outputs before sharing externally
• Update your environment profile quarterly

Treat prompts as intellectual property — your best prompts are a competitive asset.
The golden rule: Prompt engineering is not automation — it's augmented analysis. The AI amplifies the analyst. The analyst owns the decision. Responsibility does not transfer to the model.
CLOSE Key Takeaways & Next Steps 14 / 14
The 3 Things to Remember
01RCTF always. Role · Context · Task · Format. Every prompt, every time. This single habit will improve 80% of your AI interactions.
02Constrain the AI. Define what it should NOT do. Scope, tone, accuracy, authority — constraints are not limitations, they are precision tools.
03Chain and reason. Break complex tasks into steps. Ask the AI to show its work. Verify before acting. Humans stay in the loop.
Recommended Next Steps

Week 1: Each analyst picks 1 recurring task (email triage, weekly brief, alert summary) and builds a reusable prompt using RCTF.

Week 2–3: Prompt library — share best prompts in a shared SharePoint page. Start version-controlling them.

Month 2: Identify 3 use cases for chained prompting. Measure time saved vs manual workflow.

Quarter 2: Pilot adversarial simulation prompts ahead of next red team engagement. Feed MITRE ATT&CK output into detection backlog.
The goal is not to replace analysts. It is to ensure every analyst operates at their highest capability — every hour of every shift. Prompt engineering is the new power skill in cybersecurity.
